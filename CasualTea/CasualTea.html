Jin Jun Mei :::::::::::Golden Horse Eyebrow::::::::::::: Competition Grade [Yunnan Sourcing] 2018 ⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀ Top shelf black tea. It didn’t exist. There was no truly boutique black tea in China because black tea had always been viewed as inferior to greens and oolongs (which are only semi oxidized). The only reason fully oxidized teas were ever produced was so that they could endure the damp, months long journey from Canton to western nations without developing mould. Lapsang Souchoung was a tea like this. It is smoked (or as I like to say “ruined”) with pine fires to taste like car exhaust, and was very popular in the west. “Souchong” leaves are the fourth and fifth leaves of the plant, much less prized than the bud (pekoe). Tea was thousands of years old in China before black tea developed like a footnote at the end of China’s last Dynasty: the Qing. ⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀ So one of the popular black teas exported from China used inferior leaves, was smoked up like pine sol and cigar butts to be sent half way around the world - and western people still absolutely LOVED it - couldn’t get enough. Threw tantrums about getting more. Quantum leaps in sail boat speed and size still struggled to meet import demand. Eventually the Opium Wars and the British theft of tea secrets led to the start of the British/Indian tea industry, thus ending China’s dominance and monopoly in the world tea trade. Pretty wild the more you look into it, but I digress. ⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀ So, what would happen if you used the precious first pekoe leaves to make a fully oxidized black tea? That question is so obvious and absurd that no one asked it until 2005 and the answer is this damn fine amazing horse eyebrow golden twisty tiny beautiful tea. It’s made in the Wuyi mountains in the same Lapsang region and it takes thousands of pekoe buds to make one pound and gets very expensive. Wuyi rock teas, as we all remember, grow in craggy circumstances and develop complex root structures that contribute to the mineral complexity of the tea. It’s amazing that, as old as tea is, innovations of this magnitude are being made! ⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀ #chinesetea #wuyi #jinjunmei #blacktea #teasofinstagram

Shown here on my turntable bc they looked cute together. ⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀ The word “fermentation” is thrown around a lot when talking about tea processing, but what people most often mean is “oxidation” and not a process brought about by collaboration with millions of tiny microrganisms. ⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀ However, Shou Pu-Erh Tea is actually fermented in the true sense. The innovation of Shou processing was developed in the 1970’s. Shou (as opposed to the traditional Sheng) is piled in a humid environment to encourage bacteria, mold and yeasts to grow before further processing, and then finally pressed into cakes and wrapped. This process results in a tea more immediately drinkable and doesn’t require as much meticulous and careful storage as its Sheng counterpart, which ferments slowly over years/decades. Shou still loves to get old and the tastes evolve as time goes by. ⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀⠀ This tea had a very peach colored liquor and was a joy to taste. I used a little less leaf than I would for a normal session bc it was late and this stuff gets you JAZZED. I mean I get the giggles most times I drink this crazy old leaf water. Don’t forget to rinse it…twice.
